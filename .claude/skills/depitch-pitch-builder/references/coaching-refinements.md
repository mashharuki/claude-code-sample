# Coaching-Level Refinements & Design Best Practices

Apply this during Step 5: the polish pass. These are the rules I apply in 1-on-1 coaching sessions. They're the final 20% that separates a decent deck from a winning Solana pitch.

---

## SLIDE-LEVEL POLISH

### Intro slide: the 1-second test
If a stranger can't understand what you do from your intro slide in 1 second, rewrite the tagline. A precise category definition beats a generic slogan every time. Never use two brand names (company + product) on a live pitch intro, pick one.

> ❌ "AI-Based Bio Big-Data Platform"
> ✅ "We help companies discover functional plant ingredients 10x faster using AI."

### If your product has a different name than your startup, don't introduce it on slide 1
The product name means nothing until the audience understands the pain. Startup name goes on the intro; product name arrives with the solution slide, that's when it sticks.

### Background image on intro slide
Add a visual that emotionally connects your value prop to the product. The image does half the selling before you say a word.

### State your category in precise language
"Non-invasive biometric wearable for senior care" beats "healthcare monitoring system." Explain industry-specific or Solana-specific terms only if your audience is mixed (most Colosseum/Solana audiences won't need it, see Solana vocabulary rule).

## NUMBERS & DATA

### ARR vs cumulative revenue
"$500K ARR" beats "$1.1M total revenue over 3 years." Investors think in annual recurring terms. For Solana fee-based products: state ARR as daily volume × fee % × 365.

### State market size or hero metric EARLY
Lead with it: "Solana DEXs process $2B+ in daily volume" as your first sentence on the market slide. Big number hooks attention; breakdown builds credibility.

### Make key data points LARGE on the slide
Your $30K MRR, your 12K active wallets, your $9.4B market, these should be the biggest text on their slide. If the audience squints, the data point is wasted. Single most impressive number → biggest font on the slide.

### Use precise numbers, not "more than"
"$2.05M" beats "more than $2M." Precision = confidence. Hedging language on numbers signals you don't actually know your business.

### Compare your tech to products the audience knows
"40% more accurate than Apple Watch heart monitoring" is instantly understood. "Superior biometric accuracy" is not. Anchor differentiation to a household-name benchmark, for Solana products, this often means a Web2 equivalent that already proved the market.

## NARRATIVE & FLOW

### Write transition sentences between every slide
"And that's exactly why we built [product]" bridges Problem → Solution. "So how do we actually make money?" bridges Product → Business Model. **If you can't write a bridge, the slide order is wrong.**

### Reveal features one by one with animations
Don't show all features at once, the audience reads ahead and stops listening. Build step by step: click → Feature 1 → explain → click → Feature 2. Control the narrative.

### Choose positive demo scenes over negative ones
Show the AFTER state, the user smiling, the swap landing, the trade closing in profit. Joy sells better than fear.

### Combine related features into single slides
6 use cases across 3 slides → consolidate to 2 slides. Ruthlessly merge.

### Add a founding story to your team slide
"We met at [hackathon] when we both rage-quit [problem]" answers "why this team" more powerfully than any credential list.

### End with a concrete impact sentence
Not just "thank you", close with: "Every day our users save 2 hours, that's 10,000 hours saved this month alone." Or: "Last week we processed $3M in volume. Imagine what next quarter looks like."

## DEMO & VIDEO

### Embed video files directly in the deck
WiFi fails on stage. External Loom/YouTube/Vimeo links break. Embed the full video file inside the .pdf or .key/.pptx itself. Demo under 30 seconds, "wow moment" in the first 5.

### Voice over the demo yourself
Don't use pre-recorded voiceover or background music. Talk over the demo live as it plays. It keeps your connection with the audience and lets you adjust pace.

## SCRIPTING & PRACTICE

### Script the pitch to cut time in half
Founders who "wing it" routinely run 2x over time. One founder cut from 7 minutes to 3 simply by scripting. Use a timer during every practice run.

### Practice with Loom (or any screen recorder)
Record yourself, watch it back 3 times minimum before any real pitch. You'll catch filler words, trust-killing language, pacing issues, and dead slides in 5 minutes that are invisible in the moment.

### 20–40 rehearsals minimum
No DePitch alumni who won did it last-minute. The more natural it becomes, the more you can play with voice, emotion, body, and audience engagement.

### Prepare appendix slides for Q&A depth
Competitive differentiation, on-chain usage breakdowns, tokenomics math, full roadmap, financial projections, technical architecture, never in the main pitch, but ready to pull up when a judge asks. "Great question, let me show you the data on that." beats "I think we covered that earlier."

## DESIGN BEST PRACTICES

When advising on slide design:

**Do:**
- Clean layouts, bold typography, consistent color palette
- Whitespace: let the slide breathe
- One visual per slide, large key numbers
- Big tagline transitions for rhythm and impact
- One chart per slide with a clear headline stating the takeaway
- Embed demo videos directly
- Round financial figures (rounded $900k beats $923,481 for live pitches)

**Don't:**
- Busy charts with tiny fonts
- Org charts nobody can read
- Multiple competing visuals on one slide
- Walls of text
- Grey or low-contrast text, kills readability, especially in online/IR decks
- Pure black backgrounds: dark grey or dark blue looks more professional and is easier on the eyes
- Mixed visual styles across slides: looks unprofessional
- Spelling errors. One typo on a fundraise deck destroys trust ("Expension" was the actual one I saw).

**Partner logos rule:** If you show them, say something specific about each. Logos without context are decoration.

**Branded template:** Keep it consistent. A deck with mixed layouts, some slides with 80+ words next to icon-only slides, looks unprofessional. Use one template, one font system, one color palette throughout.

## STRUCTURAL TRAPS TO AVOID

### The "platform in search of a business model" trap
Many decks read like internal sales documentation or technical showcases, not a fundraise narrative. Signs you're in this trap: multiple "technology showcase" slides before any commercial proof, jargon-heavy descriptions, slides crammed with technical detail.

**Fix:** Lead with commercial proof. Bring revenue, on-chain usage, and traction forward to build credibility *before* going into the technology. Condense all technical detail into 2–3 high-impact slides max, the rest goes to appendix.

### The "multiple stories" trap
When a project has several products or markets (e.g., consumer app + B2B API + token), the deck often tries to tell three stories at once. Result: none lands.

**Fix:** Choose one dominant storyline, the most fundable one, and make the others supporting evidence. "Our consumer app drives 80% of usage. The B2B API and token are the next layers we unlock from that base."

### The "why now" gap
Many decks present a strong technology and market but never answer: why should the investor act NOW? Solana-specific tailwinds (ICM thesis maturing, mobile shipping, stablecoin volume up X% YoY, regulatory clarity in [region]) are gold but often buried in a market slide.

**Fix:** Elevate "Why Now" drivers to the Problem or Solution slide. 2–3 decisive forces: ecosystem inflection (Firedancer, mobile Chapter 2), regulatory shift, competitive whitespace, capital flow signal. Make it feel urgent, not theoretical.

### Section breaks and table of contents in a live pitch
Delete them. Flow must be continuous narrative, not chaptered like a report.

### Back-and-forth structure
problem → solution → problem → solution → customers → solution. The deck must strictly follow: problem → solution → market → traction → business case → ask. Three scattered "Problem & Solution" pages kill the narrative arc, consolidate.

### Revenue projections without context
If the audience doesn't understand your market, GTM strategy, pricing, and current traction, they can't trust any revenue projection chart. A revenue curve with no justification looks arbitrary.

### Not tailored to the audience
If you're pitching at an Asian Superteam event, show traction in Asia. Pitching to a US-focused VC? Show US users. A generic pitch signals you don't care enough. The conclusion slide can answer: "Why are you pitching THIS audience specifically?"

### Optimistic fundraising timelines
"Unicorn by 2028, deca-corn by 2030" instantly destroys credibility. Investors have seen thousands of these. Ground every milestone in real traction. "$5M ARR by Q4 2026 backed by current pipeline" is 100x more credible than a unicorn label.

### Technology section too early
Core tech slides appearing before the problem/solution/market sequence is a structural error. Investors need the simple version first, the technical deep dive later, or in appendix.

### Slide count discipline
- Max 12 slides for a 3-minute pitch
- Max 20 slides for a 7-minute pitch
- Image-only transition slides don't count against the total

### Partnership claims without stage proof
Investors will ask: "Signed contract, pilot, LOI, or just a meeting?" Always specify stage. "Active integration with Jupiter, live since [date]" beats "Working with Jupiter."

### Roadmap without measurable targets
Each milestone needs a number: "$500K ARR by Q2," "10K MAUs by Q4," "Token live with 5K holders by Q3." Vague phases ("Phase 2, scale") mean nothing.

## QUICK CHECKLIST BEFORE YOU CALL IT DONE

- [ ] Intro slide passes the 1-second test
- [ ] Hero metric is the biggest text on its slide
- [ ] No "more than," no rounded vague numbers
- [ ] Every slide has a transition sentence into the next
- [ ] Demo video embedded, not linked
- [ ] No banned uncertainty/conditional language anywhere
- [ ] CTA is a specific statement, not a vague question
- [ ] Slide count is within the time-limit cap
- [ ] Appendix slides exist for likely Q&A topics
- [ ] Total run-time tested with timer, not estimated
